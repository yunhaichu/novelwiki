## Drafting Rules

### Scene Naturalness

Before writing a line, ask whether it belongs to the scene.

Prefer:

- what the character would actually say;
- what the object would actually show;
- what the superior would actually order;
- what the low-status character would dare say;
- what the scene pressure makes necessary.

Avoid:

- author-side classification;
- polished theme summary;
- artificial map labels;
- system-like names;
- clever jokes detached from pressure;
- narration that explains the scene's meaning for the reader.

### Dialogue Pressure

Every important line of dialogue should push pressure, status, relation, information, or cost.

If a line only explains the world, asks the reader's question, or sets up a joke, cut or rewrite it.

### In-World Naming

Names should feel local.

For rough objects, use rough labels. For spoken directions, use how people in that scene would say them. Do not use polished map or system names unless the scene has a map, record, official document, or formal institution that would use that wording.

### Ending

Do not end by explaining the protagonist's thematic role.

Prefer ending on:

- an object;
- a command;
- a bodily pressure;
- a visible danger;
- a cost;
- a changed relationship;
- an action that forces the next scene.
